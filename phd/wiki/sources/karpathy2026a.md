# A Survival Guide to a PhD

By [[phd/wiki/entities/andrej-karpathy|Andrej Karpathy]]. A retrospective guide compiling tips and tricks developed during a [[phd/wiki/maps/phd|PhD]] in [[phd/wiki/concepts/computer-science|Computer Science]], [[phd/wiki/concepts/machine-learning|Machine Learning]], and [[phd/wiki/concepts/computer-vision|Computer Vision]].

## Preliminaries
Deciding to pursue a PhD involves weighing factors like freedom, ownership of research, exclusivity, status, and maximizing future choice and variance. It is a time for rapid personal growth and becoming a world expert. However, it also demands immense mental stamina, surviving isolation, and navigating unstructured environments.

## Adviser
The relationship with a PhD adviser is a symbiosis. Understanding an adviser's incentive structures (e.g., tenure track, funding, awards) helps navigate the PhD. Pre-tenure advisers are often more hands-on and intense, while post-tenure advisers operate at a higher level of abstraction. Finding the right adviser involves evaluating their position on various axes (hands-on vs. hands-off, theoretical vs. applied) and ensuring intellectual resonance. References from their current students are crucial.

## Research Topics
Research is an exercise in the [[phd/wiki/concepts/outer-loop-of-research|Outer Loop of Research]]. Instead of just solving problems (the inner loop), you must figure out what problems are ripe for solving. Developing "taste" for good problems involves:
- Finding a fertile ground with room for subsequent work.
- Aligning with your adviser's strengths.
- Being ambitious (a 10x harder problem is often approached from first principles, making it fundamentally different, not necessarily harder).
- Ensuring there is a reasonable "attack" for the problem.
- Avoiding "incremental work" (cockroach papers) that only marginally improve benchmarks without adding deep value.

## Writing Papers
Papers have a specific structure and *gestalt*. Writing should be surgically focused on a single core contribution. 
- Avoid the "laundry list" approach of just writing down everything you did chronologically.
- Adopt active, strong language (e.g., "propose" rather than "investigate").
- Set an internal deadline two weeks prior to the final submission to surface missing experiments.

## Writing Code and Giving Talks
Releasing code is highly encouraged. It forces better engineering practices (e.g., using [[phd/wiki/concepts/docker|Docker]]), reduces future maintenance pain, and increases citations. 

When giving talks, the primary goal is not to list what you did, but to get the audience excited about the problem, teach them something, and entertain them. Avoid text-heavy slides and dense result tables. 

## Attending Conferences
Conferences are primarily social events. The real action happens in the hallways, where unwritten knowledge—such as historical context, real-world method efficacy, and future directions—is shared. Becoming part of the community is vital to long-term success.

## Key claims

- The core challenge of a PhD is operating in the outer loop of research to identify worthwhile problems. ([[personal/raw/karpathy.github.io/a-survival-guide-to-a-phd#Research topics > An exercise in the outer loop.]]) — "You're not just solving problems - that's merely the simple inner loop. You spend most of your time on the outer loop, figuring out what problems are worth solving and what problems are ripe for solving."
- Academic papers should be hyper-focused on a single core contribution rather than combining multiple tangential ideas. ([[personal/raw/karpathy.github.io/a-survival-guide-to-a-phd#Writing papers > Identify the core contribution]]) — "The paper sells a single thing that was not obvious or present before... The entire paper is organized around this core contribution with surgical precision."
- Open-sourcing research code leads to better engineering practices and greater academic impact. ([[personal/raw/karpathy.github.io/a-survival-guide-to-a-phd#Writing code > Release your code]]) — "...it will force you to adopt better coding habits due to fear of public shaming... it will make others much more likely to follow up on your work"
- Tackling a massively more ambitious problem often requires a fundamental shift in strategy rather than just scaling up effort. ([[personal/raw/karpathy.github.io/a-survival-guide-to-a-phd#Research topics > Be ambitious: the sublinear scaling of hardness]]) — "If you aspire to improve something by 10% and work hard then you will. But if you aspire to improve it by 100% you are still quite likely to, but you will do it very differently."
