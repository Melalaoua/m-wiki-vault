# fgene-17-1761449

Original: [[raw/phd/fgene-17-1761449.pdf]]

This prospective, multicenter study evaluates the diagnostic contribution of [[trio-exome-sequencing]] in 86 fetuses with selected anomalies detected on ultrasound. The goal was to assess its performance compared to targeted gene panels and to better describe antenatal phenotypes for rare genetic syndromes.

### Methods
The study included ongoing pregnancies with fetal malformations strongly suggestive of a monogenic disorder, such as [[vermian-hypoplasia]], [[agenesis-of-corpus-callosum]], large hyperechogenic kidneys, and [[polymalformative-syndrome]]. All cases had a [[chromosomal-microarray-analysis]] performed. [[DNA]] from the fetus and both parents was analyzed, first with an in silico targeted gene panel and then with full [[exome-sequencing]] (ES).

### Key Findings
- **Diagnostic Yield:** Trio-ES established a definite etiological diagnosis (ACMG class 4 or 5 variants) in 28% of cases (24/86). A targeted panel identified two-thirds of these, while the remaining one-third (8 cases) were only diagnosed after extending the analysis to the whole exome. An additional 11.6% of cases had [[variants-of-uncertain-significance]] (VUSs).
- **Yield by Indication:** The success rate varied by anomaly. It was highest for vermian hypoplasia (42%), followed by polymalformative syndromes (30%), hygroma colli/generalized hydrops (25%), and large hyperechogenic kidneys (24%).
- **Genetic Landscape:** Most identified variants were missense (51%) and followed an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern (61%), with a large proportion (46%) being de novo mutations. The affected genes were primarily involved in biological functions like [[ciliopathy]] (18%), [[cytoskeleton]] (15%), and [[DNA]] metabolism (15%).
- **Notable Cases:** The study identified complex genetic situations, including a fetus with a dual diagnosis (pathogenic variants in both [[ARID1B]] and [[DCC]] genes) and another with a postzygotic somatic mosaic variant in the [[RHOA]] gene.
- **Pregnancy Decisions:** In 77% of cases where pregnancy was terminated, the decision was made before the genetic sequencing results were available, primarily based on the severity of ultrasound findings.

### Conclusion
The study confirms that trio-ES has a significant diagnostic yield of nearly 30% in selected prenatal cases, demonstrating its value over targeted panels. However, it also underscores the challenges in prenatal genetic testing, including the interpretation of VUSs, incomplete penetrance, and the difficulty of correlating genotype with an evolving fetal phenotype within the limited timeframe of a pregnancy. The authors advocate for creating a searchable clinical database to enrich antenatal data on genetic syndromes.
