Summary
Bicuspid aortic valve (BAV) is the most common congenital heart lesion in adults and is often associated with thoracic aortic aneu-
rysms and aortic stenosis. The genetic causes of most non-syndromic cases of BAV remain unknown. Pathogenic variants in
COL1A1 or COL1A2, which encode type I collagen (COL1), cause osteogenesis imperfecta (OI), a rare disorder marked by bone fragility.
Although aortic valve phenotypes, including BAV, have been described in OI, COL1 genes have not been implicated in sporadic BAV.
We report rare COL1 variants in individuals with early-onset BAV (EBAV) complications. Whole-exome sequencing was performed in
272 non-syndromic BAV probands who developed valve or aortic complications before age 30 (EBAV) and 272 biological relatives. Var-
iants were filtered by allele frequency, inheritance pattern, ClinVar classification, and in silico predictions. Participants with rare pre-
dicted damaging COL1 variants were recontacted to confirm phenotypes. Rare coding variants in COL1A1 (n = 5) and COL1A2 (n = 5)
were identified in 10 (4%) EBAV probands, representing a 30-fold enrichment compared with European ancestry populations. Only
two variants involved glycine substitutions in triple-helical domains. Affected probands presented with aortic regurgitation and/or
thoracic aneurysms requiring repair and exhibited subtle connective tissue features such as joint hypermobility, recurrent fractures,
or dental abnormalities. Predicted damaging COL1 variants are enriched in EBAV probands with features overlapping OI and
Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, though involving different residues than those in OI. Genetic testing for these variants may help identify
individuals who could benefit from personalized surveillance or targeted preventive strategies.
Introduction
Type I collagen (COL1), encoded by COL1A1 (alpha-1;
MIM: 120150) and COL1A2 (alpha-2; MIM: 120160), is
one of the most abundant proteins in the human body
and provides mechanical scaffolding for bone, skin,
tendons, arteries, and other connective tissues.1 COL1 is
synthesized as a heterotrimer of two alpha-1 chains and
one alpha-2 chain. It is highly expressed in the aortic
media and adventitia, where it contributes tensile
strength to the aorta.2 Pathogenic variants (PVs) in
COL1A1 or COL1A2 cause osteogenesis imperfecta (OI)
types I–IV, four subtypes of Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
(EDS), and COL1-related overlap disorder (C1ROD; MIM:
618172) with mixed variable features of OI and EDS.
OI refers to a group of related heritable disorders charac-
terized by bone fragility. The population prevalence of OI
is 6 per 100,000 but with substantial clinical and genetic
heterogeneity.3,4 OI type I (MIM: 166200) is the most
common subtype and the mildest form of OI.1 Individuals
with type I OI typically present due to fractures after mi-
nor trauma, with other variable findings such as blue
sclerae, hearing loss, and brittle teeth.3 Types II (MIM:
166210) and III (MIM: 259420) are considered to be the
most severe forms of OI due to spontaneous neonatal frac-
tures with substantial deformities or perinatal death.5
Type IV OI (MIM: 166220) is characterized by an interme-
diate phenotype of mild to moderate severity. While OI
primarily affects the musculoskeletal system, valvular
heart disease, myocardial dysfunction, atrial fibrillation,

The EDS subtypes are a heterogeneous group of more

some individuals with collagen PV using echocardiogra-

vascular lesions.
Bicuspid aortic valve (BAV; MIM: 109730) is the most
common congenital heart malformation (1%) and is
best characterized by autosomal-dominant inheritance of
rare variants in many different genes with incomplete
penetrance and highly variable phenotypic expressivity.
There is strong cumulative evidence that GATA4, GATA6,
NOTCH1, ROBO4, SMAD4, MUC4, and SMAD6 each
contribute to a small percentage of non-syndromic BAV
cases.14 BAV is associated with an increased lifetime risk
for TAAs (MIM: 132900), predisposing to aortic dissections
and valvular heart disease requiring surgical repair. BAV-
related complications usually increase after age 40, but
rare individuals experience significant complications as
children or young adults. We hypothesize that young
adults with early-onset complications of BAV disease
(early-onset BAV [EBAV]) represent a phenotypic extreme
enriched for rare genetic variants with large biological
effects. Consistent with this framework, we identified
recurrent rare variants of cardiac developmental genes,
including COL1A1 and COL1A2, in a cohort of individuals
selected based on EBAV phenotypes.15 Collagen variants
had not been previously implicated in familial BAV studies
or in cohorts dominated by older individuals with later-
onset disease.16 We therefore hypothesize that COL1 vari-
ants may act as genetic modifiers to accelerate the onset
or severity of BAV-associated valve and aortic disease.
Subjects and methods
The Committee for the Protection of Human Subjects at the
University of Texas Health Science Center at Houston approved
the study protocol (HSC-MS-11-0185), and all participants
provided informed consent prior to enrollment. The Genetic
Risks for Bicuspid Aortic Valve Disease study (ClinicalTrials.gov:
NCT01823432) was an international registry that collected sam-
ples and clinical data from probands with heritable non-syn-
dromic BAV disease, as well as first-degree relatives and affected
family members. Participants were enrolled at expert centers
where they received clinical care in accordance with the 2022
ACC/AHA Guideline for the Diagnosis and Management of
Aortic Disease.17
We defined early-onset complications of BAV (EBAV) as clin-
ical presentation due to moderate or severe aortic valve stenosis
or regurgitation or a large TAA (Z score > 4), or aortic valve or
aortic surgery, aortic dissection, or intervention for other
congenital left-sided lesions prior to age 30.15,18 Prospective
adult BAV cohorts are predominantly middle-aged with the
first presentation in the fourth to sixth decade of life, reflecting
the typical timing of clinically apparent valvulo-aortic disease
in adulthood. In a large population-based cohort, complex
valvulo-aortopathy, defined similarly to EBAV, accounted for
only ∼7% of adult BAV diagnoses, supporting the view that
EBAV-like phenotypes are relatively uncommon in adults.19
Aortic valve stenosis or regurgitation was defined according
to AHA/ACC guidelines.20 Individuals with known PVs, recog-
nized genetic syndromes, non-recurrent neonatal coarctation,
or complex congenital heart disease were excluded.
Whole-exome sequencing (WES) of EBAV probands, plus
parents, siblings, and any other biological relatives with BAV,
was performed at the University of Washington Center for
Rare Disease Research (UW-CRDR) as previously reported.15
Variant filtration was performed using a customized version
of seqr at the UW-CRDR (v.1.0-4cb7dfc3) with the following
settings: de novo or dominant inheritance model; pathogenic,
likely pathogenic, variants of uncertain significance [VUSs],
or conflicting ClinVar classifications; nonsense, essential
splice-site, in-frame insertion or deletion (indel), frameshift,
or missense variants; allele frequency <0.0001; REVEL >0.5;
CADD >20; genotype quality >20; and allele balance >0.25. Pre-
dicted damaging missense or in-frame indel (pDMI) variants
were retained if they were predicted to be damaging by at least
3 of PolyPhen, MutTaster, SIFT, or FATHMM.
We recontacted EBAV study participants with rare COL1 vari-
ants to complete surveys self-reporting clinical characteristics
related to OI and EDS. The assessed phenotypic features were
bone fragility, blue sclerae, long bone deformities, and dentino-
genesis imperfecta,3 defined as opalescent teeth and/or a history
of fractured teeth, as well as skin distensibility, atrophic scarring,
joint hypermobility, frequent bruising, and hernias.11 We also
screened medical records for evidence of phenotypes related to
OI or EDS.
We compared the phenotypes and genotypes of individuals
with EBAV and rare COL1 variants to a cohort of adults with types
I, III, and IV OI who were recruited into an institutional review
board (IRB)-approved study of cardiopulmonary manifestations
during routine clinic visits between August 2017 and May 2019
at the Kathryn O. and Alan C. Greenberg Center for Skeletal Dys-
plasias at the Hospital for Special Surgery and to a cohort of adults
with heritable thoracic aortic aneurysms and dissections
(HTAD).21 The HTAD dataset consists of whole-exome and
whole-genome sequences of unrelated probands with two or
more biological relatives affected by thoracic aortic disease or a

Results
Characteristics of study cohorts
The EBAV cohort included 544 individuals (98% of Euro-
pean ancestry): 272 EBAV probands, 21 relatives with
BAV, and 251 apparently unaffected family members,
including 26 trios and 15 multiplex families. All EBAV
probands were evaluated clinically to exclude genetic syn-
dromes. Detailed phenotypic features of this cohort were
previously reported.23 The average age at diagnosis was
18 years, with prevalent co-existing non-complex congen-
ital heart lesions and recurrent aortic interventions.
The OI cohort consisted of 34 individuals (40 [20–70]
years, 80% women): 20 with COL1A1 variants, 7 with
COL1A2 variants, 2 with variants in non-COL1 genes,
and 5 without genetic test results. Four individuals
(13%) had a history of cardiovascular disease: hyperten-
sion, TAAs, coronary artery disease, and/or stroke. Eigh-
teen of the 23 participants who were imaged had valve
or structural heart abnormalities such as TAAs involving
the proximal aorta, atrial septal defects, and mild or
greater valvular regurgitation. None of the individuals in
the OI cohort was reported to have a BAV. Family mem-
bers were not available for segregation analysis.
Characteristics of rare COL1 variants
Predicted damaging rare COL1A1 or COL1A2 missense
variants were identified in 10 EBAV probands (4%,
Table 1). This represents a 30-fold enrichment relative to
European ancestry gnomAD control subjects. While
EBAV variants were distributed throughout the coding
regions of COL1A1 and COL1A2, 8 of 10 were located in
the distal half of the triple-helical domains or C-terminal
propeptides (Figure 1). Most of the COL1 EBAV variants
are classified as VUSs and are associated with OI and/or
EDS phenotypes in ClinVar (Table 2).
We compared the EBAV-associated COL1 variants to
COL1 missense PV in adults with types I, II, and IV OI,
C1ROD, and HTAD. While all OI missense variants were
glycine substitutions in the triple-helical domains, only
two EBAV-associated variants were glycine substitutions
affecting COL1A2, and only one EBAV-associated variant,
c.830G>C (GenBank: NM_000089.4) (p.Gly277Ala), was
located in the triple-helical domain and is classified as
pathogenic in ClinVar. Notably, this variant was also
observed in one HTAD family with two affected individ-
uals who have the Gly277Ala variant and have BAV.
The other glycine substitution, c.3313G>A (GenBank:
NM_000089.4) (p.Gly1105Ser), is classified as a VUS.
Loss-of-function variants affecting COL1A1 (56%) and
COL1A2 (10%) were frequent in the OI cohort but absent
in the EBAV or HTAD cohort. C1ROD variants clustered in
the N-terminal region of the COL1A1 triple-helical
domain. In contrast, EBAV and HTAD variants clustered
in the C-terminal triple-helical and C-propeptide domains
of COL1A1 and the N- and C-terminal regions of the
COL1A2 triple-helical domain.
Phenotypes of individuals with rare COL1 variants
We successfully recontacted 6 of the 10 EBAV partici-
pants with rare COL1 variants. All were diagnosed with
moderate or severe aortic valve stenosis and/or aortic
valve regurgitation or large (Z > 4) TAAs (Table 2). While
none of these individuals had been diagnosed with OI,
two reported significant features that may be commonly
observed in type I OI. One EBAV proband, harboring
COL1A1 c.3278G>A (p.Arg1093His) reported a history
of fractures after minor trauma. Another EBAV proband
with COL1A2 c.3313G>A (p.Gly1105Ser) reported mod-
erate long bone deformities (bowing) and scoliosis that
did not require intervention, as well as dentinogenesis
imperfecta.
Four EBAV participants who did not have a clinical diag-
nosis of cEDS reported significant features that are associ-
ated with cEDS. One participant, who harbored COL1A2
c.3493G>A (p.Asp1165Asn) was clinically diagnosed with
cEDS and reported hypermobility-related complications
that led to corrective joint surgeries. Another proband,
with a COL1A1 c.3278G>A (p.Arg1093His) variant, reported
atrophic scarring and skin distensibility. Two other pro-
bands, harboring COL1A2 c.3490C>T (p.Arg1164Cys) and

Segregation of COL1 variants
Additional genotypes of family members were available
to determine the segregation of one rare COL1 variant
with BAV. The COL1A2 c.2122C>T (p.Arg708Trp) variant
was identified in one unaffected parent and one unaf-
fected sibling. Collectively, these observations indicate
that COL1 variants are insufficient to cause BAV in isola-
tion. Rather, they appear to act as modifier alleles, influ-
encing the severity of valvular and aortic phenotypes
and potentially accelerating the clinical recognition of
BAV-related disease.
Discussion
We analyzed WES data from 350 participants of 215 fam-
ilies to identify rare genetic variants that contribute to
early-onset complications of BAV disease. We found that
rare deleterious COL1A1 or COL1A2 variants, including
variants previously implicated in OI or EDS phenotypes,
are enriched in EBAV probands. Overall, 10 of 215 EBAV
families harbored rare COL1 variants (4%), representing a
30-fold enrichment compared to population controls.
EBAV participants harboring rare COL1 variants presented
with variable features of OI and EDS but did not exhibit se-
vere OI phenotypes such as blue sclerae, unusual fractures
or bone deformities, fragile dentition, or hearing loss.
These findings illustrate the variable penetrance and
expressivity of COL1-related conditions and highlight
potential overlaps between OI, EDS, and BAV. We found
that other recurrent EBAV-associated rare variants share
similar properties. For example, large copy number vari-
ants (CNVs) involving the 22q11.2 genomic region are en-
riched in the EBAV cohort but are not associated with most
characteristic features of the 22q11.2 deletion (MIM:
188400) or duplication (MIM: 608363) syndrome.24
Correspondingly, we observed that only one of the
10 EBAV COL1 variants was a glycine substitution in the
triple-helical domain of COL1A2. Glycine substitutions
affecting the Gly-X-Y repeats in the triple-helical domain,
or nonsense, frameshift, or splice-site PVs causing hap-
loinsufficiency of COL1A1 or COL1A2, are the most
frequent pathogenic mechanisms of COL1-related OI.25
Rare bi-allelic loss-of-function variants in COL1A2 cause
“cardiac-valvular” EDS (EDSCV), with childhood-onset
hernias and hypermobility and adult-onset cardiac valve
disease with severe mitral and/or aortic valve regurgi-
tation requiring valve replacement.26 In contrast, most
EBAV COL1 variants affected the C terminus of either
peptide or the X- or Y-positions of the triple-helical
repeats and would be expected to cause alternative or
milder phenotypes such as cEDS, aEDS, or C1ROD.27,28
One EBAV variant and several C1ROD variants are located
in the N-terminal regions of the COL1A1 or COL1A2
triple-helical domains. PVs affecting the N terminus of
COL1A1 disrupt the cleavage of the N-propeptide,
reducing the stability and tensile strength of collagen
fibrils through a different mechanism than more
C-terminal triple-helical glycine substitutions.29 The
recurrent BAV-associated Gly277Ala variant is within the

As in the general population, cardiovascular disease
is the leading cause of death for individuals with OI.
Thoracic aortic dilation and mitral or aortic valve regurgi-
tation appear to be increased in OI, but the clinical impli-
cations of cardiac findings in OI are currently unclear due
to missing data and conflicting observations.8 Notably,
life-threatening cardiovascular complications, including
arterial dissections, were reported in two small C1ROD co-
horts and may be increased in comparison to OI pheno-
types, which are considered to be milder than OI caused
by glycine substitutions in the C-terminal regions of the
triple-helical domains.25,27,28 Precise cardiovascular phe-
notyping of OI and C1ROD cohorts is needed to confirm
the prevalence of BAV and arterial aneurysms or dissec-
tions and to identify genotype-phenotype correlations
between COL1 variants and cardiovascular lesions.
COL1A1 or COL1A2 variants were not found to be
directly associated with BAV in several genome-wide asso-
ciation studies (GWASs) of older participants who pre-
dominantly presented due to aortic stenosis.16,31,32 The
phenotype of the EBAV cohort is distinct from the
GWAS cohorts due to youth (20 years at initial presenta-
tion) and valve function (predominant aortic regurgita-
tion). In this context, COL1 variants most likely function
as modifier alleles that accelerate disease onset or severity.
The milder predicted effects and gnomAD allele counts of
most EBAV-associated variants are consistent with puta-
tive roles as risk modifiers rather than causative alleles.
Disruption of the mechanical resilience of valve or aortic
tissues due to rare COL1 variants could exacerbate disease
progression in individuals with BAV or TAA by increasing
susceptibility to cytotoxic or hemodynamic stress from
smoking and hypertension, promoting earlier or more
severe manifestations of BAV disease. Common and rare
variants may influence BAV risk through shared extracel-
lular matrix pathways: three of the top seven genes iden-
tified in BAV GWASs (PALMD, TGFBI, and ADAMTSL1)
have been linked to altered COL1 deposition or organiza-
tion in valve or cardiac tissues.33–35
There was insufficient phenotypic data on EBAV fam-
ilies to perform detailed correlations between COL1 geno-
types and specific clinical features, valve configuration, or
cusp characteristics. It was not possible to determine if
rare COL1 variants segregated with BAV in all families
due to incomplete genotypic data. Some contributory
genetic variants may have been filtered out due to the
stringency of the selection criteria. This study was
restricted to adults, and additional studies are needed to
determine if COL1 variants contribute to pediatric-onset
BAV disease. The EBAV cohort was largely of European
descent, and therefore, the results of this study may not
be generalizable to non-European cohorts.
The findings of this study implicate COL1A1 and
COL1A2 as significant risk genes for EBAV disease, but
additional studies are needed to verify that COL1 rare var-
iants modify the severity of BAV-related complications. If
