# How to Do Great Work

Article by [[paul-graham]], exploring the intersection of techniques for doing [[great-work]] across various fields. 

## The Recipe for Great Work
The fundamental recipe requires choosing work with three qualities: natural aptitude, deep interest, and scope. Because ambitious people are often too conservative about scope, the primary focus should be finding something you have an aptitude for and a deep interest in. The path is guided by [[wiki/concepts/curiosity]], which acts as both the engine and the rudder.

## The Four Steps
1. **Choose a field.**
2. **Reach the frontier:** Learn enough to get to the edge of current knowledge.
3. **Notice gaps:** Knowledge expands fractally. When you get close to the edge, you'll find it is full of gaps. Notice these anomalies.
4. **Explore promising gaps:** Boldly chase outlier ideas, even if others ignore them.

## Staying Upwind and Curiosity
Most people who achieve greatness do not meticulously plan their entire path. Instead, they practice [[staying-upwind]]: at each stage, doing whatever seems most interesting and gives the best options for the future. 

## Overcoming Friction
Work has an activation energy. To overcome it, you must guard against [[wiki/concepts/procrastination]]. Graham distinguishes between per-day procrastination and **per-project procrastination**. The latter is far more dangerous because it camouflages itself as "work"—you put off your most ambitious project by industriously working on something else.

## Consistency and Exponential Growth
The cumulative effect of work is often underestimated. People who do great things get *something* done every day. Doing work that compounds leads to [[wiki/concepts/exponential-growth]]. Early on, this growth curve feels flat and is deeply underrated, requiring an extraordinary initial push to get going.

## Earnestness and Originality
To do great work, avoid affectation and strive to be earnest and intellectually honest. Originality is not a process but a habit of mind. It comes from trying to build or understand something slightly too difficult, strictness in noticing broken models, and a willingness to break rules (being [[wiki/concepts/independent-mindedness|independent-minded]]).

## School vs. Real Work
Graham critiques traditional education, noting that schools induce passivity and train students to win by "hacking the test." Real work requires figuring out what the problems are, taking risks, and unlearning the nonsense left by formal education. This fits into broader [[wiki/maps/career-strategy]].

## Morale and Colleagues
Protect your [[wiki/concepts/morale]]—it compounds alongside your work. Working on pure, difficult problems can actually serve as a refuge. Finally, seek out the best colleagues. Sufficiently good colleagues are defined by their ability to offer *surprising* insights that keep you on your toes.

## Key claims

- The foundational requirement for great work is working at the intersection of natural aptitude, deep interest, and scope. ([[raw/paulgraham.com/how-to-do-great-work#Paragraph 4 (The first step is to decide...)]]) — "The work you choose needs to have three qualities: it has to be something you have a natural aptitude for, that you have a deep interest in, and that offers scope to do great work."
- Putting off major, ambitious projects is much more dangerous than daily task avoidance because it hides behind busywork. ([[raw/paulgraham.com/how-to-do-great-work#Paragraphs on procrastination (Since there are two senses...)]]) — "Per-project procrastination is far the more dangerous. You put off starting that ambitious project from year to year because the time isn't quite right."
- People fail to invest in compounding work because exponential growth feels invisible in its earliest phases. ([[raw/paulgraham.com/how-to-do-great-work#Paragraphs on consistency and exponential growth]]) — "The trouble with exponential growth is that the curve feels flat in the beginning. It isn't; it's still a wonderful exponential curve. But we can't grasp that intuitively, so we underrate exponential growth in its early stages."
- Original ideas emerge when you stop ignoring anomalies and strictly confront broken models of the world. ([[raw/paulgraham.com/how-to-do-great-work#Paragraphs on broken models (One way to discover broken models...)]]) — "Broken models of the world leave a trail of clues where they bash against reality. Most people don't want to see these clues. [...] To find new ideas you have to seize on signs of breakage instead of looking away."
- Good colleagues are distinguished by their ability to provide surprising insights that challenge your thinking. ([[raw/paulgraham.com/how-to-do-great-work#Paragraphs on colleagues (How do you know when...)]]) — "Here's an attempt: sufficiently good colleagues offer surprising insights. They can see and do things that you can't."
